1 Section 1 · Model 1 — Weather

Can weather alone predict whether a Bordeaux vintage will be great?

Bordeaux wines are released as en primeur futures — buyers pay upfront for unfinished barrel wine, 18–24 months before delivery, based on critic barrel sample scores. The price is set after critics taste. The score is set by weather. So the question isn't just interesting — it has a financial answer.

Princeton economist Orley Ashenfelter first proved in 1988 that three weather variables predict Bordeaux vintage quality better than any wine expert. Parker called him a "total sham." History proved Ashenfelter right.

We replicated and extended his model with 43 vintages (1982–2024), using three variables measured across the growing season:

The CV R² gap (72.5% in-sample vs 48.8% out-of-sample) is meaningful — weather predicts vintage quality directionally but not precisely. The model is best understood as a signal, not a oracle. Extreme years (1991 spring frost, 2003 heatwave) fall outside what a linear weather model can capture.

Early critic commentary suggests 97–100. That gap — model vs critic — is where prestige and hype live.

2 Section 2 · Model 1 — Coefficients & Fit

How accurately does the model predict vintage scores — and where does it fail?

The regression isolates three independent signals. Growing season temperature is the dominant positive driver — warmer summers concentrate sugars and phenolics. Harvest rainfall is the biggest drag — rain dilutes and risks rot. Winter rainfall provides a deep-soil water reserve that vines draw on through the summer.

The model fails in two systematic ways. First, extreme weather events — the 1991 spring frost that destroyed 40% of buds, the 2003 European heatwave that pushed temperatures 5°C above normal — fall outside the range of normal variation the model was trained on. Second, the model predicts one score for the entire Left Bank, ignoring appellation-level microclimate variation.

3 Section 3 · The 1855 Classification

Does a 170-year-old classification system still predict château quality today?

Weather can predict average Left Bank vintage quality, but each château has its own terroir, winemaking philosophy, and accumulated reputation — none of which is easy to quantify. Across our 25 châteaux, scores range from 83–100 with average prices of $36–$469 per bottle (2017–2024).

The closest measurable proxy available from public data is the 1855 Classification — a list Napoleon III's government created to showcase French wines at the Universal Exhibition in Paris. The brokers didn't taste anything or visit vineyards. They looked at which châteaux commanded the highest prices over the previous decades and ranked them.

That ranking hasn't changed in 170 years. Except once — Mouton Rothschild was promoted from 2nd to 1st Growth in 1973, after a 20-year lobbying campaign by Baron Philippe de Rothschild.

I'll be honest — I was skeptical. Using a ranking created in 1855 by brokers who never tasted the wine feels like a joke. But when I added it to the model, R² jumped from 29.8% to 68.2%. It's outdated, politically frozen, and widely criticized by wine experts. And it still works. The most likely reason: prestige compounds over 170 years. Being a First Growth in 1855 meant higher prices, more investment, better winemaking — which made you a better First Growth in 1900, and 1950, and today. The classification doesn't measure quality. It measures accumulated advantage.

The 5th Growth finding is the most surprising: Fifth Growths score essentially the same as Third Growths — the coefficient is −0.18pts, with p=0.42. Statistically indistinguishable from zero. A classification gap that's real in price doesn't show up in quality. Collectors are paying for the tier, not the wine in the bottle.

5 Section 5 · Over & Underperformers

Which châteaux consistently outperform — and underperform — their classification?

Model 2 predicts what a château should score given the vintage quality and its 1855 tier. The residual — actual minus predicted — tells you which châteaux consistently beat expectations and which consistently disappoint.

A positive residual means the château scores above what its classification predicts. A negative residual means it underperforms. These are multi-year averages across 2000–2024, so they reflect genuine structural differences, not single-vintage noise.

Over & Underperformers — Model 2 Residuals (Avg 2000–2024)
Positive = scores above weather + classification prediction · Negative = below · Stars = >1pt from zero

Palmer (+1.68 pts) ★ — A Third Growth that performs like a First. Classified in 1855, Palmer suffered from lack of investment at the time — critics believe it would have ranked Second Growth had its potential been properly recognised. Since Thomas Duroux became winemaker in 2004, it has scored on par with Château Margaux. The +1.68 residual persists across 25 years of data. It's not noise.

Pontet-Canet (+1.50 pts) ★ — The first fully certified biodynamic château in the Médoc. After transitioning under new leadership in 2010, Pontet-Canet went from reliable Fifth Growth to occasionally matching the First Growths. Parker gave the 2010 vintage 100 points. The market now prices it like a Second Growth despite its Fifth Growth classification.

Lynch-Bages (+1.10 pts) ★ — A Fifth Growth that has quietly scored like a Third for two decades. No biodynamic certification, no obvious structural explanation. Just consistently excellent winemaking under the Cazes family.

d'Armailhac (−1.62 pts) ★ and Lagrange (−1.32 pts) ★ — Both consistently underperform their classification peers. Less critical attention, less brand investment, and the scores reflect it.

6 Section 6 · Biodynamic Farming

Does biodynamic farming produce measurably better scores and higher prices?

Biodynamic farming treats the vineyard as a self-contained ecosystem — no synthetic pesticides or fertilisers, composting, cover crops, and farming timed to lunar cycles. It goes further than organic. The theory is it produces more expressive, terroir-driven fruit.

We added a biodynamic dummy to Model 3 (the price model) — coded 1 for Pontet-Canet from 2010 and Palmer from 2017, when each received formal certification. This keeps biodynamic out of the score model (where it would reflect management decisions, not structural variables) and in the price model (where the market's reaction to biodynamic certification is exactly what we want to measure).

The finding is statistically significant (p=0.044), but the sample is limited to two châteaux. The honest interpretation: biodynamic certification adds ~28% to price, independent of the score itself. Whether that reflects genuine quality improvement or market willingness to pay for the certification story is harder to separate.

The Pontet-Canet story: Before biodynamic certification (2000–2009), Pontet-Canet averaged 92.9pts with a model residual of +1.39. After (2010–2024), it averaged 93.6pts — only a 0.7pt improvement in score. But its market price roughly doubled. The biodynamic premium is more a story the market tells itself than a measurable quality jump.
The Palmer story: Palmer went biodynamic in 2017, but had already been outperforming its classification for years before that. The +1.68pt residual persists whether we control for biodynamic or not. Palmer is exceptional for reasons that predate the certification.

8 Section 8 · Price vs Quality

Which châteaux are mispriced relative to their quality?

The model predicts price from score, vintage quality, classification, and biodynamic status. Châteaux where actual price far exceeds predicted price are carrying a prestige premium the model can't explain. Châteaux where actual price falls below predicted are potentially undervalued.

The Palmer gap: Our model predicts Palmer should cost ~$129 per bottle (2022). It sells for $435. The $306 gap isn't in the wine — it's in the name. Evidence: Palmer carries a +1.68pt residual across 25 years of real data. It's a Third Growth that scores like a First and charges like one too. The Liv-ex 2009 reclassification — based on secondary market trading prices — would classify Palmer as a First Growth. The 1855 list hasn't caught up.
The Montrose case: In 2022, Montrose scored 98pts — matching the top First Growths — and sold for $233. Model predicts ~$341. Montrose (St-Estèphe, 2nd Growth) carries less brand recognition than Pauillac peers at the same quality level. The data suggests it may be structurally underpriced relative to what the score would justify.

The ~25% MAE in Model 3 isn't a model failure — it's a feature. The part of price the model can't explain is the prestige premium. Palmer's gap ($306) is the size of that premium in dollar terms. Pontet-Canet's gap is smaller because the market hasn't fully re-priced its quality jump yet. Montrose's gap runs the other direction: quality that the market hasn't caught up to.

Nature makes the wine. Humans make the price. The gap between the two is where all the money lives.
